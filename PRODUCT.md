# Product

## Register

brand

## Platform

web

## Users

The reader is a patient, not an industry insider. Someone who has heard about Botox, microneedling, a new peptide, or a longevity clinic and wants to know, calmly and honestly, whether it is safe and worth their money. They are curious but cautious, non-clinical, and allergic to being sold to. They arrive mid-decision, often from a search result, wanting one trustworthy read rather than ten open tabs of conflicting hype. They tend to be time-poor and skim-first, so the page has to reward a thirty-second read and still hold up to a careful one.

## Product Purpose

MedSpa Daily is a daily, evidence-tiered editorial publication covering medspa studies, treatments, legislation, and longevity. It publishes one considered story per day, each one sourced and rated by evidence quality, written for the person deciding what to do with their own body and money. It exists because the aesthetics and longevity space is drowning in marketing dressed as journalism; the publication's job is to be the quiet, sourced counterweight. Success is a reader who finishes a story feeling informed rather than pitched, trusts the tier system enough to return the next morning, and comes to treat the byline as a reliable filter on a noisy field.

## Positioning

The calm, evidence-first read on the medspa world: the one publication that shows its sources and rates them, so a patient can see exactly how much to trust each claim.

## Conversion & proof

- Primary and secondary CTA: the primary action is subscribing to the daily edition so the next morning's story arrives without hunting for it. The secondary, for a visitor not ready to subscribe, is simply reading today's story or browsing a category.
- The line a visitor remembers after 10 seconds: this is the medspa read that shows its work.
- Belief ladder: before subscribing, a visitor needs to believe, in order, that this publication is independent and does not sell the treatments it covers; that every claim is sourced and openly rated by evidence quality; and that the reading itself is calm, clear, and worth a minute each morning.
- Proof on hand: the proof is structural rather than testimonial. Every story renders a visible Sources block and an evidence-tier rating; the tier system is documented publicly on the how-we-source page; the about page stands behind the house byline. There are no press mentions or reader testimonials yet at launch, and none should be implied.

## Brand Personality

Calm, considered, and literary. The voice is that of a trusted editor writing to an intelligent friend: measured, precise, never breathless, never hedging into mush. It reads like Kinfolk magazine reporting on medicine, not like a med-tech startup or a clinic's promotional site. The interface should evoke the feeling of a well-set printed weekly: unhurried, quietly confident, and honest enough to tell you when the evidence is thin.

## Anti-references

This should not look or read like a crypto-bro landing page, a glossy cosmetic-clinic site selling appointments, a cluttered health-news portal chasing clicks, or purple-gradient AI slop. No hard-sell urgency, no stock smiling-model photography, no engagement-bait headlines, no gradients or drop-shadowed cards, no dark mode with glowing accents.

## Design Principles

Text is the interface. Words carry the experience; layout, imagery, and color exist to serve the reading, never to decorate around it.

Evidence is visible. Sources and their tiers are first-class design elements, shown with confidence rather than buried in footnotes, because openness about proof is the brand promise made physical.

One unhurried thing at a time. Each screen presents a single dominant idea and lets it breathe; no competing calls to action, no infinite scroll, no manufactured urgency.

Photography breathes. Imagery is used sparingly and given generous negative space, in a muted register that reads as editorial still-life, never as clinic marketing.

## Accessibility & Inclusion

Target WCAG 2.1 AA. Body text holds at least 4.5:1 contrast against its surface and the muted secondary tone is chosen to clear that bar rather than sit just under it. The accent color is reserved for large text, rules, and non-text marks because it does not meet AA at body size. Type is set at a comfortable reading measure with generous line-height, honors the reader's font-size preferences, and all motion respects prefers-reduced-motion. The design assumes readers who may be tired, distracted, or reading on a phone in poor light, and does not rely on color alone to convey the evidence tier.
